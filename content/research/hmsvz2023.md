#### Abstract

Currencies of resource-rich economies such as Australia, Brazil, Canada, and Norway, and South Africa are often termed "commodity currencies". Dollar exchange rates of these currencies exhibit strong correlation with commodity prices. Empirically, we find that currencies with higher currency excess returns are more intensive in exporting basic commodities. However, these currencies typically crash in periods of significant negative returns to commodities, suggesting that commodity currencies inherit a risk premium due to their tendency to crash in ``disaster'' times. Using a two-country model with complete markets and disaster risk, we show that an asymmetric input-output structure of the commodity country generates a commodity currency risk premium; commodity currencies exhibit higher interest rates on average, and exhibit negative skew in excess currency returns that collapse in a disaster state.
